## Taro course: The Complete Onboarding Guide for Software Engineers

Source: Taro (jointaro.com), by **Rahul Pandey** — Taro cofounder, ex-Meta Staff SWE/Manager (also Pinterest, Kosei; career path: 4 internships → Stanford startup → Kosei (3 ppl) → Pinterest (~400) → Meta (~25k)). 3h 11m, 30 lessons, 4.98★. *This summary draws on all 30 lesson write-ups **and the full 153-slide deck**. I also fetched the video caption transcripts (~35k words) and confirmed they track the slides closely; I did not watch the videos directly.*

**The four closing takeaways:** attack onboarding with a plan · figure out your support channels · make it easy to receive help · get your hands on the keyboard.

### 1. Overview — why onboarding matters
- **Trust Meter (0–100):** you start at ~50. 0 = "immediate termination / blocked"; 100 = "run the company in perpetuity + set strategy". Raising your score unlocks larger-scoped work, and it begins in onboarding. You're *always* gaining or losing trust; trust → influence → career growth.
- **Stakes:** ~30% of employees leave within 90 days; average tech tenure 1.5–2 yrs; re-orgs are common so you'll re-onboard repeatedly. "Hire slow, fire fast."
- **Companies are bad at onboarding** — it's important-but-not-urgent, owned by no one, and docs go stale fast. Speed ranges from "here's your laptop, good luck" to "3 months of training before you can code." Poor onboarding = your opening for immediate impact.
- **Being new is a superpower** — people are nice to the newbie (so request resources aggressively), you spot problems tenured folks tolerate, you connect dots across teams. "You will never have fresh eyes again" — the superpower degrades, so act with urgency.
- **Two failure archetypes:** *Panicked noob* (tons of hours, reads everything, high stress, no focus) vs *Complacent noob* (no urgency, does only what's assigned, incurious, not enough stress). Aim between.
- **Traits of high performers:** problem-solving, communication, self-direction, drive, adaptability.

### 2. Proper onboarding mentality
- **Talk & Observe** (a loop): **Talk** → ask people their weekly/quarterly priorities, major pain points, time-spent breakdown, and who else to talk to. **Observe** → study artifacts: code contributions (authored + commented), design docs, active experiments, calendar blocks.
- Use **Boz's "Career Cold Start Algorithm"** (Meta CTO): grab 30 min with someone; ~25 min asking them to tell you everything they think you should know (copious notes, interrupt only to clarify), ~3 min on the team's biggest challenges, ~2 min on who to talk to next.
- **Create an onboarding doc** — your own reference; collaborate on it with your manager (ask questions, ask for input, write it down to remove ambiguity). Components: team charter (from company mission), partner teams + people, **measurable** expectations + milestones, project ideas with t-shirt sizing, eng rubric / career matrix. *(Maps to Saurajit's 30-day "understand the team" goal — [[people#Saurajit (my manager)]].)*
- **Have the "meta conversation"** — agree on *how* you'll work: how often to meet, how often you want feedback, written vs verbal format. Script: *"Can we talk about how you prefer us to work together over the next few weeks? I want to communicate timely updates and help requests without being disruptive."*

### 3. Asking for help
- **Bust two myths:** asking ≠ weakness (you're *expected* to ask); you won't waste their time (everyone's knowledge is incomplete).
- **Taro Expertise Theory** — you can out-know *anyone* if you bound it: within a group of **people P**, a **domain D**, and a **time T** (e.g. "more than my team of 10 about the build system, within a week").
- **Onboarding buddy** — a catch-all for norms + questions who lends you social capital; should NOT be your manager (managers can't help with in-the-weeds tooling, and the power dynamic limits candor). If none is assigned, ask for one. Spans *reactive* (answer tech qs, initial code reviews, navigate org dynamics) and *proactive* (regular check-ins, team integration via lunches/meetings).
- **Ask questions well** — a good question gets help *and* builds trust. Add context succinctly (judgment on what's relevant). Prefer **public forums** (asking one person is a single point of failure; the crowd is faster, and others benefit). Don't just say "hello" (nohello.net). *Quality 10→90 worked example:* "Hello, are you there?" (10) → "my app crashes when I clear the field" (40) → "…with a NumberFormatException" (60) → "…here's the stack trace + relevant code" (80) → "…I ruled out an NPE; I suspect string→number conversion or an EditText bug" (90).
- **Get max value per answer** — "learn to fish": ask follow-ups and understand how they got there ("what process did you use to find this fix?" beats "what's the fix?"). **Multiple-shot** questions build expertise faster than one-shot. Ask early when new (better mildly annoying for 1 month than for 6). Time-before-you-ask should rise with tenure (Month 1 learning → Month 3 building → Month 6 fully onboarded).
- **Env-setup case study** — the goal isn't the setup, it's doing what setup enables (a copy/color change). Don't try to understand everything (lean on abstraction; don't get sidetracked). When blocked, forecast ahead: pair with another new hire, keep reading the setup guide, mine error messages.
- **Failure mode — excessive handholding** — bad sign if you still need help making changes 6 months in. Healthy = your questions and code reviews *evolve* (A→B→C, not A→A'→A*). Reflect: re-read past code comments, log your questions, don't repeat mistakes, have your buddy spot patterns.

### 4. Learning the codebase
- **How NOT to learn** — exhaustively reading code + (stale) docs is inefficient. Instead dig in and code: break things, add print statements everywhere, get the debugger working, run it and watch. Hands on keyboard even when uncomfortable.
- **Pair program a lot** — learn the codebase, learn tooling/workflow quirks, and build a relationship with the paired engineer.
- **Find the 20% that matters** (Pareto) — talk to people, use git blame for hotspots, spot patterns in code reviews, look at past alerts. Randomly investigating classes is unfruitful; when new, focus on **throughput over complexity or buy-in**.
- **Rack up small wins** — "Decomposition FTW": branch → write code → deploy/test locally → submit for review → land → monitor in prod. Good first wins = small/quick changes, quality-of-life improvements, non-prod updates, knowledge sharing. Avoid = ambiguous, controversial, or multi-team refactors. (Venkat Subramaniam: "Don't walk away from complexity, run!")
- **Failure mode — fake learning** — bingeing tutorials/books/blogs feels productive (progress bars) but retains poorly. SWE is action, not theory — minimize time from reading a concept to doing it on the keyboard, inside the company codebase. Discomfort = growth.

### 5. Building relationships
- **Manager = most important relationship** (no support → no promotion). Bare minimum: recurring weekly/biweekly 1:1, 30+ min; tell them you're eager for feedback. **Keep a running 1:1 doc** (dated entries: Updates / Reflections / Action items) — preserves history + context, signals you care, and is your defense if they cancel. A career matrix structures expectations (rows like Project Impact, Direction, Engineering Excellence, People × your level vs the next).
- **Meet the team** — don't over-rely on your manager; proactively book 1:1s, recurring for close teammates early. Pre-mortem: figure out who could "throw you under the bus" and win them over. **Prefer IRL** — trust is ~10x easier in person; if hybrid, come in and align days; if remote, get to an office (company or you pays) and spend extra effort to engage.
- **Uplevel 1:1s** — three principles: pursue awkward 1:1s, go beyond status updates, write down takeaways. Topics so you never run dry: what's going well/not for you and the team, ask for opportunities/resources, give/ask feedback, brainstorm, career/perf planning. (Sample agenda mixes *fact* — what you did / will do — with *emotion* — what's top of mind, what surprised you — plus action items.)
- **Ask for specific feedback** — course-correct fast. Not "do you have any feedback?" but "**what could I start or stop doing to ramp up faster?**" Dimensions: task-specific ("one thing that would've made this stronger?"), behavioral ("something I do well I should do more of?"), meta ("best way for us to share feedback?"). Probe velocity, code quality, quality of questions. Schedule a dedicated feedback 1:1, share your own growth areas, follow up with the giver.
- **Thank people a lot** — thanks = social capital = promotion fuel. Basic = cookie-cutter 1-liner over DM; better = thank in person/call, a 1-paragraph heartfelt note, tell their manager, thank in public forums, recurring thanks (standup, team meeting, project completion). Be specific: "that unblocked a critical milestone," not "thanks for the pointer."

### 6. Contextual tips
- **Doing well?** Connect your work to the bigger picture; build a priority map. Good signs: adding value / helping newer people, improving onboarding, understanding + contributing to code reviews. Bad signs: not engaging, not getting valuable work even when you propose ideas, needing constant support. High-growth company → many neglected areas → opportunity; high-quality work → trust → bigger problems.
- **Small companies** — less process → less formal onboarding, so getting stuff done matters more; land code within a week; knowledge lives in a few heads → find the tenured people; don't assume processes/roles (QA, design) exist.
- **Large companies** — expect more process but don't let it be an excuse to move slowly (still land code within a week). Invest in relationships, understand how priorities translate by team/org, disperse/collect knowledge. Learn the jargon — e.g. Meta **PSC**, Workplace, "little r"; Amazon **LPs**; Google **GRAD**, **MK**; Microsoft **Connect**. *Directly relevant to Meta.*
- **Junior engineers** — goal is autonomy after onboarding; not expected to know much. **Coachability** is the key trait — seek feedback and act on it. Don't "solo-carry"; set milestones. Pair program.
- **Senior+ engineers** — bring domain knowledge; share opinions (contrast current vs past companies; ask why processes do/don't exist) but **"respect what came before."** Lay the foundation for team/org-level impact: build relationships and meet other senior/staff/principal engineers. *(Scope by level: L3 task · L4 feature · L5 project · L6 product/system · L7 product group/org · L8 company · L9 industry.)*
- **Probation / contract-to-hire** (common outside the US) — chat with your manager early (even before joining); call out limited info access so it doesn't reflect on you; learn the exact criteria you're judged on (commits, reviews, design docs) and track/report them.

### 7. Conclusion
- **Pass it on** — make life easier for the next hire: improve onboarding, support juniors, build more relationships, have more impact.
- **After success** — no need to memorize; revisit lessons, talk to teammates, and apply it next week.
