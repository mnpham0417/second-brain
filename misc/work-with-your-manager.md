# Working With Your Engineering Manager

**Core thesis:** Your manager is your **#1 career figure as an IC**, and the relationship is a **partnership with aligned incentives** — you both want to maximize your impact. The **onus is on you** to drive it. Think of the manager as an **API**: they won't act on their own, but call them with the right inputs and they're enormously valuable.

### 1. Overview — understanding the EM
- **What an EM is** — a leadership role supporting engineers, typically **8–12 reports**; their primary goal is that the **team consistently delivers impact**. First rung of the M-track (Director → VP → CTO above). You'll report to an EM unless you reach very senior IC (L8/L9).
- **Understand incentives** — assume people act in self-interest. Learn how your EM is evaluated so you can help them look good — which helps you.
- **EM responsibilities** — empower engineers *without* being hands-on (a hard job): growing engineers, hiring/firing + performance, clearing non-eng blockers, fostering synergy (re-orgs, people issues). Caveat: growing you is only part of it — sometimes their job is to manage someone out.
- **How EMs are evaluated** — deliver team impact and influence org direction. *Manage down*: recruit + retain effective engineers, set priorities, enable growth, coach/PIP low performers (retention is a key metric — mass attrition reads as failure). *Manage up*: frequent updates to directors/VPs, own escalations, communicate proactively.
- **Day-to-day** — meetings dominate (1:1s, leadership alignment, cross-team negotiation — much of it shielding you); needs technical depth to scope/estimate without coding; real work happens outside meetings (docs, comms, follow-up); heavy people-management and emotional load.

### 2. Approaching the relationship
- **The onus is on you** — it's not the manager's job to carry you. They're busy and context-switching; they can't read minds. Initiative is one of the traits EMs most appreciate.
- **Have a conversation about conversation** — share a **README file**: your career story, what work energizes vs drains you, what's going well/not, and how you like to receive feedback. (Same "meta conversation" idea as in [[meta-onboarding]].)
- **Be very visible** — assume your manager doesn't know what you're doing by default. Over-communicate: cc them on major updates, post milestones publicly, add them as optional to key meetings, join high-stakes meetings (e.g. SEV investigations) so they see your leadership firsthand, and surface wins/blockers in 1:1s. Communication is a top trait managers value — deliver bad news early.
- **Partner, not a boss** — relating through fear undermines trust. You share one goal: maximizing your impact (which raises their team impact). Adopt a peer/partnership mentality; you can add value to *them* too.
- **Be vulnerable** — it's your manager's job to protect your quality of life at work, but they can't if you hide problems. Good EMs act like part-time therapists. Share struggles (work or personal) *selectively* — don't overshare, don't hide everything. Vulnerability builds trust → raises the "trust meter" → unlocks higher-leverage help.
- **Feedback is a gift** — your manager is your primary (often only) source of meaningful feedback (peers avoid the discomfort). On receiving it: **say thank you, don't get defensive, reflect** even if you disagree. Most feedback has a kernel of truth — find what's useful, implement fast, follow up to show progress. Pushing back defensively can permanently shut off future feedback, leaving you "rudderless." Fastest-growing engineers treat feedback as a superpower.

### 3. Manager 1:1s
- **The basics** — gold standard: **weekly, recurring, 30 min**. Cancellations should be rare (the relationship's lifeblood). Keep a **running notes doc** (agenda, discussion, action items). 1:1s are ~1–2% of your weekly hours but influence **30–70% of how fast you grow**. *(Matches Saurajit's weekly new-hire cadence — [[people#Saurajit (my manager)]].)*
- **Before the meeting** — **seed the agenda >24h in advance** so your manager can "pre-compute" and arrive prepared. Review previous notes and follow up. Order topics by priority (critical first; overflow → Slack). A thoughtful agenda also makes it harder for them to cancel.
- **Having the meeting** — open with 2–5 min personal check-in (rapport); **share your wins** (visibility + makes perf-review writing easier; frame each with a "so what?" impact/next step); iterate through pre-seeded topics (smooth because prepared); close with **clear action items for both sides**, and start the next 1:1 by reviewing them. Take notes live — it makes the manager feel valued.

### 4. Performance review
- **Your rating should never be a (negative) surprise** — if you expect "meets" and land on a PIP, something went badly wrong, and it's almost always preventable. Regularly ask for a **hypothetical rating** ("If you had to rate me today, what would it be?" — acknowledge it isn't a guarantee, which makes them comfortable being candid). Good managers are ~**80–90% accurate** on these. Avoiding the conversation = walking yourself off a cliff.
- **Be upfront about your goals** — ratings are **human-driven and subjective**; your manager has to advocate for you, so they need to know your target ("meets" / "exceeds" / promo). **"No negative feedback" does NOT mean you're on track** — assuming so is a common, costly mistake. Even if your goal is to hold steady (e.g. 3/5), say it explicitly. Use 1:1s to set expectations and co-build a plan.

### 5. Promotion
- **Framing the dialog** — promotion needs **both** (a) you performing at the next level and (b) an **org need** for that level; great performers can still be blocked. It requires trust, so don't raise it in week one. Re-orgs derail momentum (a new manager lacks history — coordinate old + new). Promotion *signals you already operate* at higher scope/independence/cross-org trust. Frame goals around **increasing team impact**, not title/comp. Prerequisite: be a very solid performer first.
- **Dialog examples** —
  - ❌ Bad: *"I've been on this team a while and I'm stuck at the same level. Can I get promoted?"* (self-focused, transactional, no evidence, corners them into yes/no).
  - ✅ Good: *"I really enjoy working on this team. I want to do more to increase my impact and empower my teammates — what are the steps I need to take to get to that next level?"* (open-ended, team-oriented, collaborative).
- **Create a promotion plan** — get it **in writing**: a doc defining what "performing at the next level" looks like; treat it as a **living contract**, maintained monthly (priorities/re-orgs shift, especially at senior/staff where project selection matters). A plan won't guarantee promo (org politics/director calls), but it raises your odds ~**10×** vs "do good work and hope." Define the **delta** to the next level and split it by performance-review axes (anchored to behavior).
- **Good vs bad expectations** — generic career-matrix language ("have a bigger influence in code review") is useless. Make it **concrete and personalized**. Meta example: **E4** (mid) reviews code mainly within their own domain (thorough — catches architectural issues, perf, edge cases); **E5** (senior) reviews diffs **across the whole org stack** (M2+ scope), including unfamiliar areas, quickly absorbing context to leave insightful feedback — multiplicative impact. *(Directly relevant given you're joining Meta.)*
